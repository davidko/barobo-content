Preface

Welcome to "Programming the Barobo Linkbot with Python". The goals of this book are to familiarize you with the Python programming language and essential concepts of programming robots. The book accomplishes this in a "Robot-centric" manner: It teaches you how to do cool things with your Linkbot, and you learn computer science principles along the way as a side effect of making the robot do cool things. This breaks the old paradigm of teaching programming, which typically focuses on one particular core concept, such as loops or functions, per chapter. At the same time, we make sure to include all of the important computer science concepts in this book, as well as a good amount of real computer science jargon to prepare you for real-world programming.

The intended audience for this book are people with some computer experience and some basic math (Pre-Algebra) experience. The user should be familiar with the concept of files and folders, and opening and saving files, such as in Microsoft Word. Familiarity with word processing concepts, such as copy/paste, will also be extremely useful when copying large parts of code from file to file. No prior programming or robotics experience is required to follow this curriculum.

Some additional software is required for you to follow along with the lessons in this book. The required software includes Python, several third-party Python packages including our own Linkbot Control package, and a good code editor. The easiest way to obtain, install, and setup all of the software is to download and install our all-in-one Python Curriculum bundle. The bundle includes:

  • Python and all required and suggested Python packages.

  • PyScripter : A powerful and pretty looking code editor.

  • Curriculum code example programs.

  • Many other packages for numerical analysis, graphics, writing games, and much, much more.

Some users may not want to install our bundle. These include Python gurus who already have a version of Python installed, and other advanced users. For everyone else, we highly recommend you use our bundled application.

You may already have an idea of what a robot is from a variety of TV shows and movies. They vary from tiny wheeled vehicles that beep and boop to humanoid intellegent translators such as C-3PO to gigantic transforming robots like Optimus Prime. To roboticists, the definition of a robot is any machine capable of performing a programmed task and interacting with its physical environment with motors, actuators, and sensors. You may be surprised to know that, by this definition, even your refrigerator may be considered a robot! Many modern refrigerators are capable of automatically making ice, maintaining a correct amount of ice in the freezer, and even dispensing the ice at the touch of a button. This machine is running a program, uses actuators to move the ice around, and has sensors such as buttons and temperature sensors.

Indeed, robots and robotic systems have proliferated modern life, and as such, the study of robotics has become extremely important. In addition to learning about robotics for the sake of robotics, certain robots are specifically designed to help teach programming, math, science, physics, and even music! One such robot is the Linkbot modular robot. The Linkbot has been designed to be low-cost, fully-featured, tough, and easily programmable for day-to-day use by students and hobbyists of all ages, and that is the robot that the following chapters will teach you how to use.

This curriculum is designed to bring someone with limited programming experience up to speed with programming a Linkbot using the Python programming language. Python is a free, fully featured, and easy language to learn and use. It's unique design makes it popular among a large spectrum of programmers from absolute beginners to code wizards. It is commonly used as both an introductory language at universities as well as an all-purpose in-house language by many major software and technology companies.

This chapter will discuss how to operate your Linkbots, including tasks such as turning your Linkbot on and off and recalibrating your Linkbot's motors.

Figure 2.1. Linkbot Diagram and Features


Turning your Linkbot On and Off

To turn your Linkbot on and off, press and hold the power button as shown in Figure 2.1, “Linkbot Diagram and Features” . When turning the robot on, a purple LED will light up between the 'A' and 'B' buttons as soon as you press the button. Continue holding the button until that purple LED turns blue. After the LED turns blue, you may release the power button.

Turning off the robot follows a similar process. As you press and hold the power button, the LED between buttons 'A' and 'B' will turn purple. Continue holding the power button until the robot turns off all LEDs and shuts off.

Linkbot Motor Zero Positions

The Linkbot comes with a preset "Zero Position" for each of its motors. The following sections will show you how to check and set the zero position for each motor.

Moving the Motors to Zero Positions

While the robot is on, you may make the robot move its joints to its zero positions by pressing and releasing the 'A' and 'B' buttons simultaneously. The robot will move both of its motors to its zero positions and lock there. To release the joints, press the 'A' and 'B' buttons together again. This can be used to check the calibration of the motors. When the joints are in their zero positions, the large notch on the black joint face should line up with the notch on the white plastic .

If the notches do not line up after the robot moves to its zero positions, the zero positions may need to be recalibrated. The recalibration process is described in the next section.

Recalibrating the Linkbot Zero Positions

To recalibrate the Linkbot zero positions, follow the following steps:
  1. Manually move the motors to their zero positions by aligning the notch on the motors with the notch on the while plastic body. If the motors are locked, press and release buttons 'A' and 'B' simultaneously, or power-cycle the robot to release the motors.
  2. Hold down both buttons 'A' and 'B'. After about three seconds, the robot will flash its LED. It has now remembered its current motor positions as the new "Zero" position.

Linkbot Driver's Ed: Part 1

We can use the move() function to control a Linkbot's motors. 

#!/usr/bin/env python
# File: ch4ex1.py

import barobo
dongle = barobo.Dongle()
dongle.connect()

# Change the robot ID shown below to the ID of your Linkbot.
robot = dongle.getLinkbot('ABCD')
robot.setJointSpeeds(90, 90, 90)
    
robot.move(360, 0, 0)

As you may have noticed, the previous code snippet moves motor 1 one whole rotation (360 degrees) counter-clockwise, while the other motor(s) did not move at all.

Linkbot Driver's Ed: Part 2

Now, try this: 

#!/usr/bin/env python
# File: ch4ex2.py

import barobo
dongle = barobo.Dongle()
dongle.connect()

# Change the robot ID shown below to the ID of your Linkbot.
robot = dongle.getLinkbot('ABCD')
robot.setJointSpeeds(90, 90, 90)
    
robot.move(0, 0, -360)

Notice that the move() function has three arguments. Each argument controls how many degrees to move a particular motor: the first motor controls motor 1, the second controls motor 2, and the third controls motor 3. Not all motors are available on all models of Linkbot. If you have a Linkbot‑I, only motors 1 and 3 are available, so the second argument would be ignored.

Figure 3.1.  The right-hand rule.


Also note that negative values make the robot's motor spin clockwise and positive values make the motor spin counter-clockwise. The choice of negative values being clockwise and positive values being counter-clockwise follows a concept known as the "right-hand rule" and is common in the fields of math and engineering.

So, what about that Boilerplate Code?

So far, up to this point, you have been relying on BaroboBrowser to run code snippet examples for you. Now it's time to graduate you to writing your own complete Python programs that can run on any computer.[2] When you write Python programs, they should be saved as a "plain-text" file. This is the same format that Microsoft Notepad and other text editors use to save text files. The reason these files are called "plain-text" is because they are unable to store information about fonts, font sizes, page sizes, and other details that other formats, such as Microsoft Word, can store.

When you execute a Python program stored in a plain-text file, the Python interpreter scans the file, line by line, and executes the instructions written in the file. To familiarize you with this process, lets begin by playing with the Python interpreter.

Starting an Interactive Python Interpreter

The first step is starting an interactive Python interpreter. First, navigate to where you installed the Python software, as detailed in the section called “ Getting and Installing the Software ”. Open the folder, and you should see something like this:

Python Variables

Variables are elements in a computer language that are used to store and manipulate data. In most languages, variables can be used to store numbers, text strings, and other types of complex data. In Python, to create [3] variables, you simply come up with a name for your variable and give it a value. Variable names can be just about anything but they have to start with a letter or the underscore "_" character, and they can't have some special symbols in the name, such as plus signs or minus signs. Otherwise, they can contain letters, numbers, underscores. There is also a list of so-called "reserved keywords" that you cannot use as variable names because they have special meaning to the Python interpreter. The reserved keywords are: 

and      assert   break    class    continue def      del      elif   
else     except   exec     finally  for      from     global   if 
import   in       is       lambda   not      or       pass     print 
raise    return   try      while    yield

Without further ado, lets try creating a variable. Type the text "myVar = 5" into your interpreter terminal and press "Enter". Your terminal should now look like this: 

In [1]: myVar = 5

In [2]:

Now type "myOtherVar = 3": 

In [2]: myOtherVar = 3

In [3]:

Now try "myLastVar = myVar + myOtherVar": 

In [3]: myLastVar = myVar + myOtherVar

In [4]:

And finally, "print(myLastVar)": 

In [4]: print(myLastVar)
8

In [5]:

What happened here? Here's what you just did:

  • First, you created a variable called myVar and gave it the value "5".
  • Next, you created a variable myOtherVar and gave it the value "3".
  • Then, you created a variable called myLastVar. Its value is the result of the calculation myVar + myOtherVar
  • Finally, with the print() function, you printed the value of myLastVar, which is 8.

As you can see, you can use variables to store numbers and also to calculate values. Now, lets try another slightly more interesting example: 

In [1]: x = 5

In [2]: x = x + 3

In [3]: print(x)
8

In [4]:

Check out line 2. If you've taken Algebra, this line might look very weird to you. With code and calculations like this, it is much better to think of the equal sign as an assignment operator rather than equality, which you may be used to from math. In other words, Python interprets this line as "Calculate the value of x + 3. Take the result and assign it to the variable x".

Using the Python Interpreter as a Calculator

Python supports all of the math operations you might be used to on your calculator, although some of the symbols they use might be different than what you are used to on pencil and paper. Here are some common math operations you can use in Python:

  • + : Addition
  • - : Subtraction
  • * : Multiplication
  • / : Division
  • ** : Exponent e.g. 5**2 is "five squared"
  • ( ... ) : Precedence. e.g. (4+3)*2

Note that you can just type the calculation right on the interpreter terminal and it will automatically show you the result. 

In [4]: ((5+4)*4)**3
Out[4]: 46656

In [5]:

Note that this is a special feature of the Python interpreter. When you start writing Python programs in files, you will either need to store the result in a variable or print it out with the print() function.

So, what about that Boilerplate Code?

We mentioned at the beginning of last section that we were only going to show you some abbreviated pieces of code. If you were curious, you may have expanded the code examples and looked at the whole unabridged Python code. What you saw was probably something like this: 

 
#!/usr/bin/env python

# File: ch4simple.py
# A simple and complete Python program that moves the motors on a Linkbot

import barobo

dongle = barobo.Dongle()
dongle.connect()
linkbot = dongle.getLinkbot('ABCD')

# Move all joints by 90 degrees in the positive direction
linkbot.move(90, 90, 90)

You may recognize the last line of that piece of code from the previous chapter and the previous section. It is the piece of code that actually makes the robot move. Lets take a closer look at this code, one line at a time.

The first line looks like this: 

 #!/usr/bin/env python

This line tells the computer that the program is a Python program. You should remember to put this line at the top of all of your Python programs. Many different languages have some sort of boilerplate code; some more complex than others. The next two lines look like this: 

# File: ch4simple.py
# A simple and complete Python program that moves the motors on a Linkbot

Note that these lines and the first line of code begin with the '#' character. In programmer-speak, this character is usually referred to as a "pound-sign" or a "hash" character. This character has special meaning in Python. It tells the Python interpreter to ignore anything else that comes after it in the line. This is known as a comment . Comments are text that are meant tell a human reader some information but are ignored by the computer as the computer executes the program.

Next, we see 

import barobo

The import command tells Python to import a package. Python packages are code libraries written by people to perform tasks that are not part of Python's default functionality. For instance, there are packages specifically designed to handle graphics, or play sounds, or perform highly complex number-crunching functions. Here, we import a package called "barobo", which is a package specially designed to manage and control Barobo robots, including the Linkbot.

Once a package is imported, you can access its contents by specifying its name. For instance, consider the next line of code in the sample program: 

dongle = barobo.Dongle()

The code barobo.Dongle() creates a Barobo Dongle object and stores it inside the variable dongle using the assignment operator, "=". This is a special type of complex variable that is neither a number nor a string. The dongle object has a list of its own functions that can be executed to perform tasks. For instance, the next line, 

dongle.connect()

connects to your Barobo Dongle. (If you do not have a Barobo Dongle attached to your computer when you execute this line, you might receive an error message)

The next line, 

linkbot = dongle.getLinkbot('ABCD')

tells the dongle object to find a Linkbot with the serial ID "ABCD" and assign it to the variable "linkbot".

These three lines are required to to get control of a Linkbot with the serial ID "ABCD". Of course, if you decide to try and execute this code, you will probably want to change "ABCD" to the serial ID of your own Linkbot.
